What Is E-E-A-T? The Full Breakdown

Before diving into strategies, let’s clarify what E-E-A-T actually means—because many marketers misinterpret it.

1. The Evolution of E-A-T to E-E-A-T

Google’s original E-A-T (Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) framework was introduced in the 2018 Search Quality Evaluator Guidelines. However, in 2022, Google expanded it to E-E-A-T by adding "Experience" as a fourth pillar.

Why the change? Google wanted to better evaluate content quality, especially for Your Money or Your Life (YMYL) topics (health, finance, legal, safety, etc.). Now, personal experience (like a doctor writing about medical conditions) carries more weight than just expertise alone.

2. The Four Pillars of E-E-A-T (With Examples)

Pillar Definition Example of Strong E-E-A-T Example of Weak E-E-A-T
Expertise The qualifications, skills, or knowledge of the content creator. A certified financial advisor writing about retirement planning. A blogger with no finance background giving tax advice.
Authoritativeness The reputation and influence of the website or author in their field. WebMD ranking #1 for medical queries due to decades of credibility. A new blog with no backlinks or citations ranking for "best running shoes."
Trustworthiness The accuracy, transparency, and reliability of the content. Mayo Clinic citing peer-reviewed studies in their health articles. A fake news site claiming a "miracle cure" with no sources.
Experience The firsthand knowledge the author has of the topic. A former nurse writing about hospital safety protocols. A non-medical writer reviewing a new drug without real-world use.

3. How Google Evaluates E-E-A-T (The Hidden Signals)

Google doesn’t publicly disclose all its ranking factors, but Search Quality Evaluator Guidelines (updated in 2023) provide key insights:

  • Content Quality & Purpose – Is the content helpful, original, and well-researched?
  • Author Credentials – Does the author have real-world expertise?
  • Website Reputation – Does the site have strong backlinks, reviews, and citations?
  • User Engagement – Does the content solve problems (low bounce rates, high dwell time)?
  • Transparency & Disclosures – Are affiliate links, sponsorships, and biases clearly stated?

Pro Tip: Google’s John Mueller has confirmed that E-E-A-T is a "tiebreaker"—if two pages are equally good, the one with stronger E-E-A-T ranks higher.

How to Measure Your E-E-A-T Score (Tools & Metrics)

Before optimizing, you need to audit your current E-E-A-T. Here’s how:

1. Expertise Checklist

✔ Do your authors have:

  • Degrees, certifications, or professional licenses?
  • Public speaking engagements, podcast appearances, or media features?
  • A track record of writing in the niche?

Tools to Check:

  • LinkedIn, Google Scholar, or industry directories (e.g., Healthgrades for doctors, BBB for businesses).
  • Ahrefs or Moz to see if your authors are cited as sources elsewhere.

2. Authoritativeness Checklist

✔ Does your website have:

  • High-quality backlinks from authoritative sites (e.g., NYTimes, Forbes, academic journals)?
  • A strong Google Business Profile (for local SEO)?
  • Positive reviews on Trustpilot, Yelp, or industry forums?

Tools to Check:

  • Ahrefs’ "Domain Rating" (DR) or Moz’s "Domain Authority" (DA).
  • Google Search Console’s "Links" report to see who links to you.
  • Review aggregators like Trustpilot or G2 for user feedback.

3. Trustworthiness Checklist

✔ Is your content:

  • Fact-checked (e.g., Snopes, FactCheck.org)?
  • Transparently sourced (citing studies, experts, or official data)?
  • Free from misleading claims (e.g., false testimonials, exaggerated benefits)?

Tools to Check:

  • Google’s "About This Result" feature (click the dropdown under search results).
  • Fact-checking tools like NewsGuard or PolitiFact.
  • Manual review of disclaimers, affiliate links, and sponsorships.

4. Experience Checklist

✔ Do your authors have:

  • Firsthand experience in the topic (e.g., a former athlete reviewing fitness gear)?
  • Case studies or real-world examples in their writing?
  • A personal story or testimonial that adds credibility?

Tools to Check:

  • Author bios (do they mention past jobs, projects, or challenges they’ve faced?).
  • Social media profiles (LinkedIn, Twitter, or industry forums).

🚀 Quick Audit Tip: Use Google’s "Helpful Content Update" self-assessment:

  1. Is your content primarily written for users or for SEO?
  2. Does it provide unique insights or just regurgitate existing info?
  3. Would a real person find it helpful?

If the answer is no, your E-E-A-T is likely weak.

8 Actionable Strategies to Boost Your E-E-A-T in 2024

Now that you know how to measure E-E-A-T, let’s dive into practical strategies to improve yours.

Strategy 1: Build a Team of True Experts (Not Just Writers)

Problem: Many websites hire generalist writers who lack deep niche knowledge, hurting expertise.

Solution: Hire or collaborate with real experts in your industry.

How to Implement:

✅ For B2B & SaaS:

  • Partner with industry consultants (e.g., Salesforce experts writing about CRM trends).
  • Interview CEOs, founders, or senior executives for exclusive insights.
  • Offer guest posts to thought leaders in exchange for high-authority backlinks.

✅ For YMYL Topics (Health, Finance, Legal):

  • Hire licensed professionals (e.g., doctors for medical content, CPAs for tax advice).
  • Use "Expert Contributor" programs (like Harvard Business Review’s "HBR Guest Articles").
  • Create an "Advisory Board" of industry leaders to review your content.

Real-World Example: HubSpot’s Content Strategy

HubSpot doesn’t just write about marketing—it features real marketers in its blog. For example:

  • "How I Grew a SaaS Company from $0 to $1M MRR" (written by a real founder).
  • "The Psychology of Persuasion in Email Marketing" (co-written with a behavioral psychologist).

Result: HubSpot’s blog ranks #1 for 100+ high-volume keywords because it combines expertise + real-world experience.

Strategy 2: Optimize Author Bios for Maximum Credibility

Problem: Many websites have generic bios like: "Jane Doe is a passionate writer who loves SEO."

Solution: **Make bios specific, authoritative, and trustworthy.

How to Implement:

✅ Include:

  • Degrees, certifications, or licenses (e.g., "MD, Harvard Medical School").
  • Past roles & achievements (e.g., "Former Head of Growth at [Company]").
  • Public speaking engagements (e.g., "Spoke at SXSW 2023 on AI in Marketing").
  • Media features (e.g., "Quoted in Forbes, TechCrunch, and The Wall Street Journal").
  • A professional photo (Google trusts faces—studies show 75% of users judge credibility based on author photos).

✅ Example of a Strong Bio:

"Dr. Emily Carter, PhD in Nutrition (Stanford), is a registered dietitian with 15 years of experience helping patients with metabolic disorders. She’s been featured in The New York Times, WebMD, and Prevention Magazine, and has spoken at TEDx and the American Society for Nutrition Conference. Currently, she serves as a nutrition advisor for the WHO’s global obesity initiative."

Real-World Example: The New York Times’ Health Section

Every NYT health article includes:

  • The author’s full credentials.
  • A link to their professional profile (if applicable).
  • A photo (to humanize the expert).

Result: Readers trust NYT health content 9x more than generic blogs.

Strategy 3: Use Schema Markup to Signal E-E-A-T to Google

Problem: Google can’t always read your content—it needs structured data to understand who wrote it and why they’re credible.

Solution: Implement schema markup to explicitly tell Google about your authors’ expertise.

How to Implement:

✅ Key Schema Types for E-E-A-T:

  1. Person Schema – For authors.
    {
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "Person",
  "name": "Dr. Emily Carter",
  "jobTitle": "Nutrition Expert & Registered Dietitian",
  "affiliation": "Stanford University",
  "alumniOf": {
    "@type": "EducationalOrganization",
    "name": "Stanford University"
  },
  "worksFor": {
    "@type": "Organization",
    "name": "World Health Organization"
  }
}
  2. Author Schema – For content attribution.
    {
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "BlogPosting",
  "author": {
    "@type": "Person",
    "name": "Dr. Emily Carter",
    "sameAs": "https://linkedin.com/in/dr-emily-carter"
  }
}
  3. Review Schema – For product/service reviews.
    {
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "Review",
  "author": {
    "@type": "Person",
    "name": "John Smith",
    "jobTitle": "Former Product Tester at TechReview"
  },
  "reviewRating": {
    "@type": "Rating",
    "ratingValue": "5",
    "bestRating": "5"
  }
}
  4. FAQPage Schema – For Q&A content (helps with featured snippets).
    {
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "FAQPage",
  "mainEntity": [
    {
      "@type": "Question",
      "name": "How do I improve my E-E-A-T score?",
      "acceptedAnswer": {
        "@type": "Answer",
        "text": "By hiring experts, using schema markup, and getting backlinks from authoritative sites."
      }
    }
  ]
}

✅ Tools to Implement Schema:

  • Google’s Structured Data Markup Helper (free).
  • Schema App or Mersey Interactive (paid, but powerful).
  • Yoast SEO (WordPress plugin) for basic schema.

Real-World Example: Moz’s Use of Schema

Moz uses Person schema for its SEO experts, like:

{
  "@type": "Person",
  "name": "Rand Fishkin",
  "jobTitle": "Founder of Moz & SparkToro",
  "alumniOf": {
    "@type": "EducationalOrganization",
    "name": "University of Michigan"
  }

Result: Google displays author bios in search results, increasing click-through rates (CTR) by 20%.
