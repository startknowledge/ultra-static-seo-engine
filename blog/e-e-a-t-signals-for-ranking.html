What Is E-E-A-T? The Full Breakdown

Before diving into strategies, let’s clarify what E-E-A-T really means—because many marketers still confuse it with EEAT (without the extra "E") or misapply it.

1. Expertise: Proving You Know Your Stuff

Expertise means demonstrating deep, specialized knowledge in your niche. Google doesn’t just want content—it wants content from true experts.

How Google evaluates expertise:

  • Author credentials (Are they a doctor, lawyer, or industry veteran?)
  • Depth of knowledge (Do they cover advanced topics, not just surface-level info?)
  • Consistency in content quality (Do they cite sources, update regularly, and avoid misinformation?)

Example: A finance blog written by a certified financial planner (CFP) with real-world case studies will rank higher than a generic article from an unknown author.

2. Authoritativeness: Being Recognized as a Leader in Your Field

Authoritativeness goes beyond expertise—it’s about being seen as a trusted source by other industry leaders, media outlets, and users.

How Google evaluates authoritativeness:

  • Backlinks from high-DA sites (Are reputable sites linking to you?)
  • Mentions in news/media (Are you quoted in industry publications?)
  • Social proof (Do influencers and experts reference your work?)

Example: A health blog that gets featured in WebMD or Harvard Health will have stronger authority than one with no external recognition.

3. Trustworthiness: Ensuring Accuracy and Transparency

Trustworthiness is about proving your content is reliable, unbiased, and free from deception.

How Google evaluates trustworthiness:

  • Transparency (Do you disclose conflicts of interest?)
  • Accuracy (Do you cite credible sources?)
  • User experience (Is your site secure, fast, and free of spam?)

Example: An online pharmacy review site that clearly states its revenue model (e.g., "We earn commissions from affiliate links") builds trust, while one that hides its affiliations risks penalties.

4. Experience: The New "E" in E-E-A-T (2024 Update)

Google has expanded E-E-A-T to include "Experience"—meaning firsthand knowledge matters just as much as expertise.

Why Experience is critical:

  • Health & safety topics (e.g., "How to treat a burn") require real-life experience, not just theoretical knowledge.
  • Personal finance & legal advice benefits from practical insights from people who’ve been in similar situations.

Example: A travel blog written by someone who has actually visited every destination they recommend will rank higher than one based on generic research.

The 10 Most Actionable E-E-A-T Strategies for Ranking in 2024

Now that we understand what E-E-A-T is, let’s dive into 10 proven strategies to strengthen your signals and climb the search rankings.

Strategy 1: Build a Strong Author Bio (With Credentials & Real Photos)

Why it works: Google’s Search Quality Rater Guidelines emphasize that authors should be identifiable with real-world credentials.

How to implement: ✔ Use a professional photo (No stock images—Google wants to see a real person) ✔ Include full credentials (Degrees, certifications, years of experience) ✔ Link to LinkedIn or professional profiles (For extra authority) ✔ Write in first person (e.g., "As a 10-year veteran in SEO, I’ve seen...")

Real-World Example: Neil Patel’s blog uses detailed author bios with:

  • Photos of the team
  • LinkedIn profiles
  • Certifications & achievements This boosts trust and reduces bounce rates, signaling to Google that the content is credible.

Common Mistake: ❌ Using a generic "About Us" page with no individual bios. ✅ Fix: Assign unique bios to every author—even for guest contributors.

Strategy 2: Publish Firsthand Experience-Based Content

Why it works: Google’s 2024 updates prioritize content with real-world experience, especially in health, finance, and DIY niches.

How to implement: ✔ Write about your own experiences (e.g., "How I Fixed My Car’s Transmission in 3 Days") ✔ Include personal anecdotes (e.g., "After trying 5 protein powders, this is what worked for me") ✔ Use case studies (e.g., "How I Lost 20 lbs in 3 Months—My Exact Routine")

Real-World Example: The Minimalists (famous blog) ranks #1 for "minimalist lifestyle" because their content is based on their own 10+ years of minimalist living, not just theory.

Common Mistake: ❌ Writing generic guides without personal insight. ✅ Fix: Add a "My Story" section to every relevant post.

Strategy 3: Get Featured in Industry Publications & Media

Why it works: Backlinks from high-authority sites (like Forbes, HuffPost, or niche industry blogs) directly boost E-E-A-T.

How to implement: ✔ Pitch guest posts to reputable sites in your niche ✔ Offer expert quotes to journalists (Use HARO—Help a Reporter Out) ✔ Create shareable infographics that get picked up by media ✅ Example Pitch:

"Hi [Editor’s Name], I noticed your recent article on [topic]. As a [your title] with [X years] experience, I’d love to contribute an expert perspective. Here’s a draft: [link]."

Real-World Example: Ahrefs grew its brand authority by getting featured in TechCrunch, The Verge, and Forbes, leading to higher rankings for competitive keywords.

Common Mistake: ❌ Spamming low-quality directories for backlinks. ✅ Fix: Focus on earned media—high-quality mentions > spammy links.

Strategy 4: Use Schema Markup to Highlight Expertise

Why it works: Schema markup helps Google understand your content’s credibility by tagging authors, credentials, and expertise.

How to implement: ✔ Add Author schema to blog posts ✔ Use FAQPage schema for Q&A content ✔ Mark up Review and Product pages with expert ratings

Example Schema Code (JSON-LD):

<script type="application/ld+json">
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "Article",
  "author": {
    "@type": "Person",
    "name": "Jane Doe",
    "jobTitle": "Certified Financial Planner (CFP)",
    "sameAs": "https://linkedin.com/in/janedoe"
  },
  "publisher": {
    "@type": "Organization",
    "name": "Ultra-Static-SEO",
    "logo": "https://ultra-static-seo.com/logo.png"
  }
}
</script>

Real-World Example: WebMD uses schema markup to highlight doctor credentials, making their content more trustworthy in Google’s eyes.

Common Mistake: ❌ Not using schema at all. ✅ Fix: Implement schema for authors, reviews, and FAQs to boost E-E-A-T signals.

Strategy 5: Create "How-To" & "Step-by-Step" Guides with Real Proof

Why it works: Google loves detailed, actionable guides—especially when they include real-world proof.

How to implement: ✔ Break down processes (e.g., "How to Start a Dropshipping Business in 7 Days") ✔ Include screenshots/videos (if allowed) of your own steps ✔ Add "I Tried This Myself" sections

Real-World Example: Backlinko’s "Ultimate Guide to SEO" ranks #1 for "SEO guide" because it combines theory with real case studies from Brian Dean’s 10+ years of experience.

Common Mistake: ❌ Writing theoretical guides without proof of execution. ✅ Fix: Add a "My Results" section to show real outcomes.

Strategy 6: Disclose Affiliate Relationships & Conflicts of Interest

Why it works: Google penalizes hidden affiliate links—but transparency builds trust.

How to implement: ✔ Add a clear disclaimer (e.g., "This post contains affiliate links. We may earn a commission.") ✔ Label sponsored content (e.g., "Sponsored: [Brand] paid for this review") ✔ Use "No Follow" links where appropriate

Real-World Example: Wirecutter (NY Times) ranks #1 for product reviews because it fully discloses affiliate relationships while prioritizing user needs.

Common Mistake: ❌ Hiding affiliate links in fine print. ✅ Fix: Be upfront—transparency = trust.

Strategy 7: Get Testimonials & Case Studies from Real Users

Why it works: Social proof (testimonials, case studies) proves your expertise in action.

How to implement: ✔ Ask happy clients for video testimonials ✔ Publish case studies (e.g., "How We Helped [Client] Increase Sales by 300%") ✔ Feature user-generated content (e.g., "Our Readers’ Success Stories")

Real-World Example: HubSpot’s blog ranks high for "marketing strategies" because it shares real client success stories alongside expert advice.

Common Mistake: ❌ Using fake testimonials. ✅ Fix: Only feature real, verifiable success stories.

Strategy 8: Update Old Content with New Expert Insights

Why it works: Google favors fresh, updated content—especially when it adds new expertise.

How to implement: ✔ Audit old posts (Use Ahrefs or SEMrush to find outdated content) ✔ Add new data, case studies, or expert quotes ✔ Update stats (e.g., "In 2023, X% of businesses use this strategy—here’s how")

Real-World Example: Backlinko’s "SEO Ranking Factors" is constantly updated with new data, keeping it #1 for years.

Common Mistake: ❌ Leaving old posts stagnant. ✅ Fix: Refresh content every 6-12 months with new insights.

Strategy 9: Build a Strong "About Us" Page with a Clear Mission

Why it works: Your About Us page is Google’s first impression of your brand’s trustworthiness.

How to implement: ✔ Explain your expertise (e.g., "We’re a team of 5 CFPs with 20+ years combined experience") ✔ Show your mission (e.g., "Our goal is to help small businesses avoid financial mistakes") ✔ Include team bios (Not just a generic "We’re passionate about SEO")

Real-World Example: Neil Patel’s About Us page clearly states his credentials and why he’s an authority, making his site more trustworthy.

Common Mistake: ❌ Having a weak, generic About Us page. ✅ Fix: Make it engaging, credible, and mission-driven.

Strategy 10: Monitor & Respond to User Feedback (Reviews & Comments)

Why it works: Engaging with users (via comments, reviews, and social media) shows Google that your content is valuable and trustworthy.

How to implement: ✔ Encourage comments (e.g., "What’s your experience with this? Leave a comment!") ✔ Respond to negative reviews (Shows you care about user satisfaction) ✔ Use Google Reviews to highlight positive feedback

Real-World Example: SEMrush’s blog ranks high because it actively engages with readers in comments and responds to feedback.

Common Mistake: ❌ Ignoring comments and reviews. ✅ Fix: Be proactive—show users (and Google) that you value their input.
