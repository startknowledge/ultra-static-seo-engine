What Is E-E-A-T? A Deep Dive into Google’s Quality Rater Guidelines

Before diving into strategies, let’s clarify what E-E-A-T really means—because many marketers misinterpret it as just "having experts write content."

1. Experience (E)

Google doesn’t just want any experience—it wants firsthand, relevant experience that aligns with the topic.

  • For YMYL topics (e.g., medical advice, financial planning):
    • A doctor should write about symptoms, not a random blogger.
    • A certified accountant should explain tax deductions, not a freelance writer.
  • For non-YMYL topics (e.g., product reviews, hobby guides):
    • Personal experience (e.g., a tech reviewer who actually uses the product) carries weight.
    • Industry experience (e.g., a former chef writing about kitchen gadgets) is valuable.

Example: A personal finance blog ranking for "Best Retirement Accounts for Millennials" would benefit from authors who have personally managed retirement funds—not just generic financial advisors.

2. Expertise (E)

This isn’t about having a degree—it’s about demonstrating deep knowledge in a subject.

  • For technical topics (e.g., SEO, coding):
    • Certifications, patents, or published research help.
    • Contributions to open-source projects or speaking at conferences boost credibility.
  • For creative fields (e.g., design, writing):
    • Portfolios, case studies, or client testimonials prove expertise.
    • Awards or industry recognition (e.g., "Best Web Designer of 2023") adds weight.

Example: A SEO agency ranking for "How to Rank a New Website in 2024" should feature authors with:

  • Google Search Central certifications
  • Years of hands-on SEO experience
  • Case studies showing real ranking improvements

3. Authoritativeness (A)

This is about being recognized as a leader in your niche.

  • Backlinks from high-authority sites (e.g., Forbes, NY Times, industry-specific publications).
  • Mentions in major media outlets (e.g., being quoted in a Wall Street Journal article).
  • Being cited in academic or professional research (e.g., a study referencing your work).

Example: A health blog ranking for "Best Supplements for Joint Pain" would benefit from:

  • Links from Mayo Clinic or WebMD
  • Features in health magazines like Men’s Health or Prevention
  • Citations in medical journals

4. Trustworthiness (T)

This is the final hurdle—Google wants to ensure your content is accurate, transparent, and free from bias.

  • Clear author bios with credentials and experience.
  • Transparency in affiliate marketing (e.g., disclosing commissions).
  • Fact-checking and sourcing (citing studies, expert interviews, or official data).
  • No misleading claims (e.g., "This supplement cures cancer" without evidence).

Example: A finance blog ranking for "Best Credit Cards for Travel" must:

  • Disclose affiliate partnerships (e.g., "We earn commissions from card providers").
  • Provide real user reviews (not just manufacturer claims).
  • Update outdated information (e.g., if a card’s rewards change).

8 Actionable Strategies to Boost Your E-E-A-T for Higher Rankings

Now that we understand what E-E-A-T is, let’s explore how to implement it in your ultra-static SEO strategy.

Strategy 1: Build a Strong Author Authority System

Google loves when content is attributed to real experts. A single generic author bio won’t cut it—you need a structured authority system.

How to Do It:

✅ Create dedicated author pages with:

  • Full name, professional title, and credentials
  • Headshot (if appropriate for your niche)
  • LinkedIn or professional portfolio links
  • A brief bio explaining their expertise

✅ Use "About the Author" sections at the end of long-form content.

✅ Feature guest contributors from high-authority sources (e.g., industry experts, researchers).

Real-World Example:

Medscape (medical news site) ranks #1 for thousands of medical queries because:

  • Every article is written by licensed doctors.
  • Each author has a detailed bio with credentials.
  • The site cites peer-reviewed studies in every post.

Mistake to Avoid: ❌ Using "John Doe" as an author—Google can’t verify expertise. ❌ Not updating author bios when credentials change.

Strategy 2: Optimize for "People Also Ask" (PAA) with Expert Answers

Google’s People Also Ask (PAA) section is a goldmine for E-E-A-T signals because it prioritizes authoritative, expert-driven answers.

How to Do It:

✅ Research PAA questions in your niche (use AnswerThePublic, AlsoAsked, or Google’s search suggestions). ✅ Answer them in-depth with:

  • Data-backed insights (studies, surveys, expert opinions).
  • Clear, structured formatting (bullet points, tables, step-by-step guides).
  • Citations (links to sources).

✅ Use schema markup (FAQ schema) to help Google understand your answers.

Real-World Example:

WebMD dominates health-related PAA questions because:

  • Their answers are written by doctors and nurses.
  • They cite medical studies (e.g., "According to the CDC, 80% of migraines are triggered by stress").
  • Their FAQ schema is properly implemented.

Mistake to Avoid: ❌ Copying answers from forums (Reddit, Quora) without adding value. ❌ Not updating outdated information (e.g., citing a 2010 study for a 2024 topic).

Strategy 3: Leverage Expert Roundups & Interviews

Expert roundups (where multiple authorities answer a question) signal high E-E-A-T because: ✔ Multiple perspectives validate the topic. ✔ Each expert brings unique credibility. ✔ Backlinks from their sites boost authority.

How to Do It:

✅ Find 5-10 experts in your niche (use LinkedIn, Twitter, or industry directories). ✅ Ask them a specific question (e.g., "What’s the biggest SEO mistake in 2024?"). ✅ Publish their answers in a dedicated roundup post (with proper attribution).

✅ Promote the roundup to experts for backlinks and social shares.

Real-World Example:

HubSpot’s "Marketing Experts Share Their Best Advice" posts rank well because:

  • They feature top marketers (e.g., Neil Patel, Rand Fishkin).
  • Each expert provides unique, actionable insights.
  • The post gets shared widely, increasing authority.

Mistake to Avoid: ❌ Using fake or low-quality experts (Google can detect this). ❌ Not giving proper credit (plagiarism hurts trust).

Strategy 4: Publish Case Studies & Data-Driven Content

Case studies and data-driven content are the strongest E-E-A-T signals because they prove real-world results.

How to Do It:

✅ Document real client successes (if you’re an agency). ✅ Conduct original research (surveys, experiments, A/B tests). ✅ Use visuals (charts, graphs, infographics) to present data clearly.

✅ Cite sources (e.g., "Based on our analysis of 1,000 websites, X% improved rankings").

Real-World Example:

Backlinko’s "How We Ranked a Client in 30 Days" case study ranks #1 because:

  • It shows step-by-step results (before/after metrics).
  • It cites real data (e.g., "Traffic increased by 420%").
  • It includes screenshots of Google Analytics.

Mistake to Avoid: ❌ Making up numbers (Google’s algorithm can detect inconsistencies). ❌ Not disclosing limitations (e.g., "This worked for our niche, but results vary").

Strategy 5: Build High-Quality Backlinks from Authoritative Sources

Backlinks are still the #1 ranking factor—but not all links are equal. Google prioritizes links from high-E-E-A-T sites.

How to Do It:

✅ Get featured in industry publications (e.g., Forbes, Entrepreneur, niche blogs). ✅ Contribute guest posts to authoritative sites in your niche. ✅ Earn links through original research (studies, surveys, tools).

✅ Avoid spammy links (PBNs, paid links, or low-quality directories).

Real-World Example:

Ahrefs’ "Backlink Analysis Tool" ranks #1 because:

  • They get links from tech sites (TechCrunch, Wired).
  • Their content is cited in SEO guides (e.g., Moz, Neil Patel’s blog).
  • They publish original research (e.g., "The State of Backlinks in 2024").

Mistake to Avoid: ❌ Buying links (Google penalizes this). ❌ Over-optimizing anchor text (e.g., "best SEO agency" everywhere).

Strategy 6: Use Schema Markup to Enhance E-E-A-T Signals

Schema markup helps Google understand your content better, which boosts E-E-A-T perception.

How to Do It:

✅ Use Author Schema (for blog posts, articles). ✅ Use FAQ Schema (for question-answer content). ✅ Use HowTo Schema (for step-by-step guides). ✅ Use Review Schema (for product/service reviews).

Real-World Example:

Yelp’s restaurant reviews rank #1 because:

  • They use Review Schema to highlight ratings.
  • They attribute reviews to real users (not bots).
  • They display expert endorsements (e.g., "Chef-approved").

Mistake to Avoid: ❌ Using incorrect schema types (e.g., putting FAQ schema on a product page). ❌ Not testing schema validity (use Google’s Rich Results Test).

Strategy 7: Update & Refresh Old Content for Accuracy

Stale content hurts E-E-A-T because Google prioritizes fresh, accurate information.

How to Do It:

✅ Audit old posts (use Ahrefs, SEMrush, or Google Search Console). ✅ Update statistics, studies, and examples with new data. ✅ Add new sections if the topic has evolved. ✅ Fix broken links (404s hurt trust).

Real-World Example:

HubSpot’s "Inbound Marketing Guide" ranks #1 because:

  • They update it annually with new trends.
  • They remove outdated strategies.
  • They add new case studies.

Mistake to Avoid: ❌ Leaving old, incorrect information (e.g., citing a 2015 study for a 2024 topic). ❌ Not updating author bios if credentials change.

Strategy 8: Implement Transparency in Affiliate & Sponsored Content

Google penalizes hidden affiliate deals and misleading sponsored content. Full transparency builds trust.

How to Do It:

✅ Disclose affiliate relationships (e.g., "This post contains affiliate links"). ✅ Label sponsored content (e.g., "Sponsored by [Brand]"). ✅ Be honest about biases (e.g., "We only recommend products we’ve personally used").

Real-World Example:

Wirecutter (NY Times) ranks #1 for product reviews because:

  • They disclose affiliate partnerships upfront.
  • They test every product themselves.
  • They avoid bias (e.g., no free samples from brands).

Mistake to Avoid: ❌ Hiding affiliate links (e.g., "Shop Now" buttons without disclosure). ❌ Accepting free products without disclosure.
