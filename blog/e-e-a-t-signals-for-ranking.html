What Is E-E-A-T? The Full Breakdown

Before diving into optimization strategies, it’s essential to understand what E-E-A-T actually means and how it differs from the older E-A-T framework.

E-A-T vs. E-E-A-T: The Key Difference

Google introduced E-A-T (Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) in its 2018 Search Quality Evaluator Guidelines. However, in 2022, Google expanded this to E-E-A-T, adding "Experience" as a critical factor—especially for Your Money or Your Life (YMYL) topics (health, finance, legal advice, etc.).

Factor Definition Why It Matters in 2024
Experience Firsthand knowledge or real-world application of the topic. Google now prioritizes personal experience (e.g., a doctor writing about medical conditions vs. a layperson).
Expertise Deep knowledge in a specific field (education, certifications, professional background). Authority is judged by credentials, not just popularity.
Authoritativeness Recognition as a leader in your industry (backlinks, citations, media mentions). External validation (like being quoted in reputable sources) boosts rankings.
Trustworthiness Transparency, accuracy, and ethical practices (no misleading claims, clear disclaimers). Google penalizes low-trust sites—especially in YMYL niches.

Why Google Emphasizes E-E-A-T in 2024

Google’s 2023 Search Quality Update reinforced that E-E-A-T is now a primary ranking signal, particularly for:

  • Health & Wellness (e.g., fitness advice, medical treatments)
  • Finance & Legal (e.g., tax strategies, investment advice)
  • Safety & Security (e.g., home security tips, cybersecurity guides)
  • Local Businesses (e.g., restaurants, service providers)

A 2024 Moz study found that websites with strong E-E-A-T signals rank an average of 2.5 positions higher than competitors who ignore these factors.

8 Actionable Strategies to Strengthen Your E-E-A-T Signals

Now that you understand what E-E-A-T is, let’s explore how to implement it in your SEO strategy.

Strategy 1: Build a Strong Author Bio (With Credentials & Experience)

Why it works: Google’s 2023 guidelines explicitly state that authors should be identifiable with real-world expertise.

Example:

  • Before: A blog post about "Best Running Shoes for Plantar Fasciitis" has no author bio.
  • After: The same post includes:

    "Written by Dr. Emily Carter, a board-certified podiatrist with 15 years of experience in sports medicine. She has treated over 5,000 athletes and is a contributing expert to the American Podiatric Medical Association."

How to implement: ✔ Include a detailed author bio under every article. ✔ List credentials (degrees, certifications, years of experience). ✔ Add a professional headshot (if applicable). ✔ Link to LinkedIn or a professional profile (if relevant).

Real-world example:

  • WebMD uses MDs and nurses to write health articles, clearly displaying their credentials.
  • The New York Times features journalists with bylines, including their expertise (e.g., "Science Reporter, Covering Climate Change").

Strategy 2: Leverage Firsthand Experience (Especially for YMYL Topics)

Why it works: Google’s 2024 updates favor personal experience over generic advice.

Example:

  • Before: A fitness blogger writes about "How to Lose Weight Fast" without mentioning their own weight loss journey.
  • After: The same post includes:

    "I lost 50 pounds in 6 months using this method—here’s how I did it, step by step."

How to implement: ✔ Write about topics you’ve personally experienced (if possible). ✔ Include case studies or personal anecdotes in your content. ✔ For non-personal topics, cite real-world studies or expert interviews.

Real-world example:

  • Mark’s Daily Apple (a nutrition blog) often includes personal stories from the founder, Mark Sisson, about his own health transformations.
  • The Wirecutter (a product review site) tests products themselves before recommending them, adding firsthand experience to their reviews.

Strategy 3: Get Featured in Reputable Publications (Backlinks & Authority Boost)

Why it works: External backlinks from authoritative sites signal authoritativeness to Google.

Example:

  • Before: A finance blog has no external mentions.
  • After: The same blog is quoted in Forbes, CNBC, or The Wall Street Journal.

How to implement: ✔ Pitch guest posts to industry-leading sites. ✔ Get interviewed by podcasts or news outlets. ✔ Contribute to whitepapers or industry reports. ✔ Use HARO (Help a Reporter Out) to get media mentions.

Real-world example:

  • Neil Patel has been featured in The New York Times, Entrepreneur, and Forbes, boosting his authority in SEO and digital marketing.
  • Gary Vaynerchuk frequently appears on CNN, Bloomberg, and The Tonight Show, reinforcing his expertise in business and marketing.

Strategy 4: Use Schema Markup to Highlight Expertise & Credentials

Why it works: Schema markup helps Google understand your content’s credibility better.

Example:

  • Before: A doctor’s blog post doesn’t use schema.
  • After: The same post includes:
    {
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "MedicalProfessional",
  "name": "Dr. Sarah Johnson",
  "hasCredential": {
    "@type": "EducationalOccupationalCredential",
    "name": "Board-Certified Physician",
    "recognizedBy": "American Board of Internal Medicine"
  }
}

How to implement: ✔ Add "Person" schema for authors. ✔ Use "EducationalOccupationalCredential" schema for degrees/certifications. ✔ Implement "FAQPage" schema for expert-backed answers.

Real-world example:

  • Mayo Clinic uses schema markup to display doctor bios, credentials, and patient reviews, reinforcing trust.
  • WebMD marks up medical professionals’ qualifications, making it clear who’s writing the content.

Strategy 5: Create In-Depth, Research-Backed Content (Not Just Thin Content)

Why it works: Google penalizes low-quality, thin content—especially in YMYL niches.

Example:

  • Before: A 300-word blog post on "How to Fix a Leaky Faucet" with no sources.
  • After: A 2,000-word guide with:
    • Step-by-step instructions
    • Video tutorials
    • Links to plumbing studies
    • Expert interviews

How to implement: ✔ Cite reputable sources (studies, government data, industry reports). ✔ Include original research (surveys, case studies, data analysis). ✔ Update old content with new findings.

Real-world example:

  • HowStuffWorks provides deep dives into complex topics with visuals, expert quotes, and citations.
  • The Atlantic publishes long-form investigative journalism with footnotes and sources, reinforcing credibility.

Strategy 6: Build a Strong Brand Authority (Beyond Just Backlinks)

Why it works: Brand recognition (social media, press mentions, awards) signals authoritativeness.

Example:

  • After: The same agency:
    • Wins awards (e.g., "Best SEO Agency 2024" by Search Engine Land).
    • Has a strong LinkedIn presence with 50K+ followers.
    • Is quoted in major publications.

Real-world example:

  • Ahrefs is frequently cited in SEO blogs and has strong backlinks from top tech sites.

Strategy 7: Optimize for User Trust Signals (Transparency & Disclaimers)

Why it works: Google penalizes misleading or unethical content—especially in YMYL topics.

Example:

  • Before: A supplement blog makes unproven health claims without disclaimers.
  • After: The same blog includes:

    "This content is for informational purposes only and not meant to replace medical advice. Always consult a doctor before starting new supplements."

How to implement: ✔ Add disclaimers for sensitive topics. ✔ Be transparent about affiliate partnerships. ✔ Correct mistakes quickly (if you publish wrong info).

Real-world example:

  • Healthline includes clear disclaimers and sources all claims.
  • Consumer Reports tests products independently and discloses funding sources.

Strategy 8: Use Expert Contributors & Collaborate with Industry Leaders

Why it works: Featuring experts in your content boosts credibility.

Example:

  • Before: A tech blog writes about AI trends without expert input.
  • After: The same blog includes interviews with AI researchers from Stanford and MIT.

How to implement: ✔ Invite industry experts to contribute guest posts. ✔ Host webinars or podcasts with thought leaders. ✔ Feature expert quotes in your articles.

Real-world example:

  • TechCrunch frequently interviews CEOs and investors from Silicon Valley.
  • Harvard Business Review publishes articles by top professors and executives.

Common E-E-A-T Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

Even well-intentioned SEO strategies can backfire if E-E-A-T is ignored. Here are 5 deadly mistakes and how to fix them.

Mistake 1: Ignoring Author Bios (Or Using Fake Ones)

Problem: Many blogs don’t credit authors, making content seem untrustworthy.

Solution: ✔ Always include a real author bio with credentials. ✔ Avoid "John Doe" or generic names—use real names and photos.

Example:

  • Before: A blog post has no author info.
  • After: The post includes:

    "This article was written by Sarah Johnson, a certified financial planner with 10 years of experience at XYZ Wealth Management."

Mistake 2: Relying on Generic Advice Without Personal Experience

Problem: Non-experts writing about sensitive topics (health, finance, law) can harm readers.

Solution: ✔ Only write about topics you have real experience in. ✔ If you don’t have experience, cite experts (studies, interviews).

Example:

  • Before: A non-doctor writes about "How to Cure Diabetes."
  • After: The same post includes:

    "While I’m not a doctor, I’ve interviewed Dr. Michael Green, a diabetes specialist at Johns Hopkins, who confirms these are the most effective treatments."

Mistake 3: Not Updating Old Content (Stale Information = Low Trust)

Problem: Outdated content makes your site look unreliable.

Solution: ✔ Review old posts every 6–12 months. ✔ Update statistics, studies, and expert quotes.

Example:

  • Before: A 2018 post on "Best Running Shoes" still says Nike Air Max is the best.
  • After: The updated post includes:

    "Since 2018, new research shows that Hoka Bondi shoes have better cushioning for long-distance runners. We’ve updated our recommendations accordingly."

Mistake 4: Overlooking Schema Markup (Google Can’t Read Your Credentials)

Problem: Even if you have credentials, Google may not recognize them without schema.

Solution: ✔ Implement "Person" schema for authors. ✔ Use "EducationalOccupationalCredential" schema for degrees.

Example:

  • Before: A doctor’s blog has no schema.
  • After: The site includes:
    {
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "MedicalProfessional",
  "name": "Dr. Robert Smith",
  "hasCredential": {
    "@type": "EducationalOccupationalCredential",
    "name": "MD, Harvard Medical School"
  }
}
