What Is E-E-A-T? The Full Breakdown of Google’s Trust Framework

Before diving into strategies, let’s clarify what E-E-A-T actually means—because many marketers still get it wrong.

1. Experience (The Often Overlooked "E")

Google’s 2022 update expanded E-E-A-T to include Experience, making it E-E-A-T (not just E-A-T). This means:

  • Firsthand experience in the topic (e.g., a personal injury lawyer writing about medical malpractice vs. a general blogger).
  • Real-world application (e.g., a fitness coach who’s lost 50+ pounds vs. someone who’s just read about diets).
  • User-generated content (e.g., customer reviews, case studies, and testimonials).

Why it matters: Google now prioritizes content from people who have lived the experience—not just armchair experts.

2. Expertise (Proving You Know Your Stuff)

Expertise isn’t just about having a degree—it’s about demonstrating deep knowledge in your niche.

How Google evaluates expertise: ✔ Education & certifications (e.g., a CPA writing about taxes vs. a random blogger). ✔ Professional affiliations (e.g., American Medical Association (AMA) members in health content). ✔ Public speaking & media appearances (e.g., a finance expert quoted in Forbes). ✔ Published works (e.g., books, research papers, or industry reports).

Example: A dental clinic’s blog ranking for "best teeth whitening" will outperform a random beauty blog if the dentist has published studies on whitening techniques and cites peer-reviewed research.

3. Authoritativeness (Being Recognized as a Leader in Your Field)

Authoritativeness is about being seen as a trusted source by other experts and platforms.

How to build authority: ✔ Backlinks from high-DA sites (e.g., NY Times, Harvard, or industry-specific publications). ✔ Featured in reputable directories (e.g., Yelp, BBB, or Google Business Profiles). ✔ Being cited in other authoritative content (e.g., Wikipedia, academic journals, or government sites). ✔ Hosting webinars, podcasts, or conferences (e.g., HubSpot Academy, Coursera, or industry summits).

Real-World Example: HubSpot’s blog ranks #1 for "inbound marketing" because:

  • They cite studies from MIT Sloan in their content.
  • They’re featured in Harvard Business Review.
  • Their co-founders are industry thought leaders with TEDx talks and bestselling books.

4. Trustworthiness (Making Users & Google Feel Safe)

Trustworthiness is the final piece—if users don’t trust your site, Google won’t rank it, no matter how strong your other signals are.

How to build trust: ✔ Secure website (HTTPS) – Google penalizes unsecured sites. ✔ Transparent business practices (e.g., clear refund policies, privacy policies, and contact info). ✔ Accurate, up-to-date information (e.g., no outdated medical advice). ✔ Positive user reviews & testimonials (e.g., Google Reviews, Trustpilot, or case studies). ✔ No deceptive practices (e.g., clickbait, fake testimonials, or misleading ads).

Example of a Trust Issue: A finance blog that promises "get rich quick" schemes but has no real case studies will lose trust signals and rank poorly, even if it has some expert contributors.

The 10 Most Actionable E-E-A-T Strategies to Rank Higher in 2024

Now that we understand what E-E-A-T is, let’s dive into 10 proven strategies to strengthen your signals and outrank competitors.

Strategy 1: Optimize Author Bios for Maximum Impact

Why it works: Google’s 2022 update made author bios a ranking factor—especially for YMYL topics. A strong author bio signals expertise, experience, and trustworthiness.

How to do it right: ✅ Include real credentials (e.g., "Dr. Jane Smith, MD, Board-Certified Cardiologist"). ✅ Link to professional profiles (LinkedIn, Google Scholar, or industry certifications). ✅ Showcase experience (e.g., "15+ years in pediatric dentistry"). ✅ Use structured data (Schema.org Person or Organization markup).

Example: A health blog about "best supplements for ADHD" should feature an author like:

*"Dr. Michael Chen, PhD – Licensed Psychologist & ADHD Specialist Founder of NeuroFocus Clinic | Author of The ADHD Brain Blueprint | Featured in Psychology Today & Harvard Health Publishing"

Mistake to avoid: ❌ Generic bios like "I love writing about health!" (Google ignores these).

Strategy 2: Leverage Firsthand Experience (The "E" in E-E-A-T)

Why it works: Google prefers content from people who have lived the experience—not just theoretical knowledge.

How to implement: ✅ Write about personal stories (e.g., "How I Recovered from Chronic Pain"). ✅ Include case studies (e.g., "Client X Lost 100 lbs Using My Method"). ✅ Use "I" statements (e.g., "I struggled with insomnia for years until I tried this..."). ✅ Cite real-world data (e.g., "Based on my 500+ clients, here’s what works").

Example: A weight loss coach could write:

"I tried every diet under the sun—keto, intermittent fasting, even the cabbage soup diet—until I found a method that actually worked for me. Here’s how I lost 60 lbs in 6 months without starvation."

Why this works:

  • Proves experience (not just theory).
  • Builds trust (users see a real person, not a faceless expert).
  • Encourages engagement (people love relatable stories).

Strategy 3: Get Featured in High-Authority Publications

Why it works: Backlinks from authoritative sites are one of the strongest E-E-A-T signals. If Forbes, Harvard, or the BBC links to you, Google knows you’re trustworthy.

How to get featured: ✅ Pitch guest posts to industry blogs (e.g., Entrepreneur, Mashable, or niche publications). ✅ Contribute to Wikipedia (if you’re an expert in your field). ✅ Get quoted in news articles (use HARO—Help a Reporter Out). ✅ Publish research or whitepapers (e.g., "The State of [Industry] in 2024").

Example: A marketing agency could:

  1. Write a guest post for HubSpot’s blog on "AI in SEO."
  2. Get quoted in a Forbes article on "The Future of Digital Marketing."
  3. Publish a whitepaper on "Local SEO Trends 2024" and distribute it to media outlets.

Result:

  • More backlinks (boosting authority).
  • More social proof (proving expertise).
  • Higher rankings in competitive keywords.

Strategy 4: Use Schema Markup to Highlight E-E-A-T Signals

Why it works: Schema markup helps Google understand your content better, making it easier to rank for E-E-A-T-related queries.

Key Schema Types for E-E-A-T:

Schema Type Example Use Case
Person Author bios, credentials, experience
Organization Business details, awards, affiliations
Review Customer testimonials, ratings
FAQPage Answering common questions with expert-backed responses
HowTo Step-by-step guides with expert validation

Example: A legal blog about "divorce laws in California" could use:

<script type="application/ld+json">
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "FAQPage",
  "mainEntity": [
    {
      "@type": "Question",
      "name": "What are the grounds for divorce in California?",
      "acceptedAnswer": {
        "@type": "Answer",
        "text": "California is a no-fault divorce state, meaning you don’t need to prove wrongdoing. Grounds include 'irreconcilable differences' or 'incurable insanity.' **Source: California Family Code § 6501.**"
      }
    }
  ]
}
</script>

Why this helps:

  • Google displays FAQs in rich snippets (increasing CTR).
  • Citations (like "Source: California Family Code") boost trust.
  • Structured data signals expertise to Google.

Why it works: Certifications are direct proof of expertise—Google loves when you showcase them.
