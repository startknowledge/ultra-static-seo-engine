What Is E-E-A-T? A Deep Dive into Google’s Trust Framework

Before diving into strategies, let’s clarify what E-E-A-T really means—because many marketers still misunderstand it.

The Original E-A-T (Now E-E-A-T) Breakdown

Google’s E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) was first introduced in its 2018 Quality Rater Guidelines. The "E" originally stood for Experience, but in 2022, Google expanded it to Expertise (now Experience + Expertise) to better reflect the depth of knowledge required for certain topics.

Here’s the updated breakdown:

Signal Definition Why It Matters to Google
Experience The real-world knowledge the author or site has about a topic. Google prioritizes firsthand experience (e.g., a doctor writing about medical conditions vs. a layperson).
Expertise The depth of knowledge demonstrated in content. High expertise = better problem-solving for users.
Authoritativeness The recognition a site or author has in their field (e.g., citations, awards, media mentions). Authority = social proof that your content is credible.
Trustworthiness The reliability of your site (e.g., transparency, accuracy, security). Trust = lower bounce rates and higher conversions.

Why the "E" Was Split (And What It Means for You)

Google’s 2022 update split the first "E" into two distinct signals:

  • Experience → Real-world application (e.g., a chef writing about cooking techniques they’ve used).
  • Expertise → Formal or deep knowledge (e.g., a PhD in neuroscience writing about brain health).

Why the change? Google wanted to distinguish between: ✔ Opinion-based content (e.g., a blogger’s personal fitness routine). ✔ Evidence-based content (e.g., a certified nutritionist’s meal plans).

For SEO, this means:

  • Low-experience, low-expertise content (e.g., thin affiliate reviews) will struggle to rank.
  • High-experience, high-expertise content (e.g., case studies from industry leaders) will rank higher.

The 10 Most Powerful E-E-A-T Signals (With Actionable Strategies)

Now that we’ve established what E-E-A-T is, let’s explore the 10 most impactful signals you can implement today to improve your rankings.

1. Author Bios & Credentials (The "Expertise" Signal)

Why it works: Google’s 2022 update made it clear—anonymous authors hurt rankings. A well-crafted author bio signals expertise and trustworthiness.

How to Optimize for E-E-A-T:

✅ Include:

  • Name, title, and professional credentials (e.g., "Dr. Jane Smith, MD, Harvard Medical School").
  • A brief but detailed bio (3-5 sentences) explaining their expertise and experience.
  • Links to professional profiles (LinkedIn, academic publications, industry certifications).

❌ Avoid:

  • Generic bios like "I love writing about SEO."
  • Missing photos or fake credentials.

Real-World Example: How The New York Times Uses Author Bios

The NYT doesn’t just list authors—they highlight their credentials in a way that instantly builds trust:

"Dr. Sanjay Gupta, MD, is Chief Medical Correspondent for CNN and a neurosurgeon at Emory University. He has authored multiple books on health and medical ethics."

Action Step:

  • Audit your author bios—if they’re missing credentials, update them immediately.
  • Use structured data (Schema.org/Person) to enhance visibility in search results.

2. Firsthand Experience (The "Experience" Signal)

Why it works: Google prefers content from people who have lived the experience they’re writing about.

How to Optimize for E-E-A-T:

✅ Showcase real-world experience in:

  • Case studies (e.g., "How I grew my SaaS from $0 to $1M in 12 months").
  • Personal anecdotes (e.g., "As a former software engineer at Google, I know these 5 bugs kill your app performance").
  • Interviews with experts (e.g., "We asked 10 cybersecurity pros about the biggest 2024 threats").

❌ Avoid:

  • Generic advice (e.g., "Here are 10 ways to improve SEO" without proof).
  • Overly promotional content (e.g., affiliate links without context).

Real-World Example: How NerdWallet Uses Experience

NerdWallet doesn’t just review credit cards—they include real user testimonials and expert insights:

"After testing 20+ travel credit cards, we found the Chase Sapphire Preferred offers the best rewards for frequent flyers—here’s why."

Action Step:

  • Add a "Why I Wrote This" section in your intro (e.g., "I’ve been a personal finance coach for 10 years, so I know these mistakes hurt your credit score").
  • Use "We" instead of "You" in some sections to personalize the experience.

3. Citations & Backlinks from Authoritative Sources (The "Authoritativeness" Signal)

Why it works: Backlinks from trusted sites (e.g., .edu, .gov, industry leaders) boost authority.

How to Optimize for E-E-A-T:

✅ Build citations by:

  • Getting featured in industry publications (e.g., Forbes, Harvard Business Review).
  • Contributing to academic journals (if applicable).
  • Earning backlinks from high-DA sites (use Ahrefs, Moz, or SEMrush to find opportunities).

❌ Avoid:

  • Low-quality backlinks (e.g., spammy directories).
  • Over-optimized anchor text (e.g., "best SEO agency"—Google penalizes this).

Real-World Example: How HubSpot Builds Authority

HubSpot doesn’t just write blogs—they get cited by major outlets:

"According to HubSpot’s 2024 State of Marketing Report (cited by Forbes), 73% of marketers say AI improves content quality."

Action Step:

  • Pitch guest posts to authoritative sites in your niche.
  • Leverage HARO (Help a Reporter Out) to get media mentions.

4. Transparency & Disclosures (The "Trustworthiness" Signal)

Why it works: Hidden agendas hurt trust. Google penalizes sites that don’t disclose:

  • Affiliate relationships
  • Sponsored content
  • Potential biases

How to Optimize for E-E-A-T:

✅ Include:

  • Affiliate disclosures (e.g., "This post contains affiliate links—we may earn a commission").
  • Sponsored content labels (e.g., "This article was sponsored by [Brand]").
  • Correction policies (e.g., "If we make an error, we’ll update this post").

❌ Avoid:

  • Buried disclosures (e.g., in the footer).
  • False claims (e.g., "Our product is FDA-approved" when it’s not).

Real-World Example: How Wirecutter Handles Disclosures

The New York Times’ Wirecutter is the gold standard for transparency:

"We independently select and review everything we recommend. If you buy through our links, we may earn a commission."

Action Step:

  • Add a "Disclosures" page linking to all relevant posts.
  • Use Schema.org/Disclosure for structured data.

5. High-Quality Content Depth (The "Expertise" Signal)

Why it works: Thin, fluffy content gets demoted. Google prefers in-depth, well-researched articles.

How to Optimize for E-E-A-T:

✅ Make content:

  • At least 1,500+ words (for competitive topics).
  • Backed by data (studies, case studies, expert quotes).
  • Structured with subheadings, bullet points, and visuals (even if you don’t use images, descriptive alt text helps).

❌ Avoid:

  • Listicles (e.g., "10 Ways to Lose Weight" without depth).
  • Repurposed content (e.g., copying from Wikipedia).

Real-World Example: How Backlinko Dominates with Depth

Backlinko doesn’t just write about SEO—they dive deep with data-backed insights:

"Our 2024 study of 1 million Google search results shows that page speed impacts rankings more than most SEOs realize."

Action Step:

  • Use the "Skyscraper Technique"—find top-ranking content, improve it, and outdo it.
  • Add a "Sources" section at the end of long-form posts.

6. User Engagement & Dwell Time (The "Trustworthiness" Signal)

Why it works: If users stay longer, Google assumes your content is trustworthy.

How to Optimize for E-E-A-T:

✅ Boost engagement by:

  • Answering questions directly (use FAQ Schema).
  • Encouraging comments (moderate them to keep discussions relevant).
  • Adding interactive elements (e.g., quizzes, calculators).

❌ Avoid:

  • Clickbait titles (e.g., "You Won’t Believe What Happens Next!").
  • Low-quality comments (e.g., spam, off-topic replies).

Real-World Example: How Lifehacker Keeps Users Engaged

Lifehacker encourages interaction with:

  • "What’s your experience?" sections.
  • Polls and surveys embedded in articles.

Action Step:

  • Run a heatmap analysis (using Hotjar or Crazy Egg) to see where users drop off.
  • Add a "Was this helpful?" CTA at the end of posts.

7. Security & HTTPS (The "Trustworthiness" Signal)

Why it works: Unsecured sites (HTTP) get penalized—Google explicitly states that HTTPS is a ranking factor.

How to Optimize for E-E-A-T:

✅ Ensure:

  • SSL certificate (use Let’s Encrypt for free).
  • No mixed content warnings (all links must be HTTPS).
  • Regular security audits (use Sucuri or Wordfence).

❌ Avoid:

  • Outdated security plugins.
  • Self-signed certificates.

Real-World Example: How Amazon Uses HTTPS

Amazon never uses HTTP—every page loads over HTTPS, reinforcing trust.

Action Step:

  • Check your site with SSL Labs (https://www.ssllabs.com/ssltest/).
  • Redirect all HTTP traffic to HTTPS in your server config.

8. Social Proof & Community Endorsements (The "Authoritativeness" Signal)

Why it works: If others vouch for you, Google trusts you more.

How to Optimize for E-E-A-T:

✅ Build social proof by:

  • Getting featured in podcasts (e.g., "We were interviewed on the Smart Passive Income Podcast").
  • Earning awards (e.g., "Best SEO Agency of 2024").
  • Showcasing testimonials (e.g., "98% of our clients see a 30%+ increase in organic traffic").

❌ Avoid:

  • Fake testimonials.
  • Overhyped claims without proof.

Real-World Example: How SEMrush Uses Social Proof

SEMrush highlights awards and media mentions on their homepage:

"Trusted by 10M+ marketers worldwide | Featured in Forbes, TechCrunch, and The Wall Street Journal"

Action Step:

  • Create a "Press & Awards" page.
  • Add testimonials in your footer.
