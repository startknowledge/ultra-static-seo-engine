Introduced by Google’s Search Quality Evaluator Guidelines (most recently updated in 2023), E-E-A-T isn’t just a set of vague principles—it’s a direct ranking signal that determines whether your content earns top positions in search results.

  • Google’s 2023 Search Quality Update reinforced E-E-A-T as a primary factor in evaluating Your Money Your Life (YMYL) topics (health, finance, legal advice, etc.).

What Is E-E-A-T? The Full Breakdown of Google’s Trust Framework

Before diving into strategies, let’s clarify what E-E-A-T actually means and how Google evaluates it.

1. Experience (E)

Definition: The firsthand knowledge or real-world interaction a creator has with the topic they’re writing about.

Why It Matters: Google doesn’t just want generic content—it wants authentic, practical insights from people who’ve lived, worked, or struggled with the subject.

Example:

  • A blog post about "How to Fix a Leaky Faucet" written by a plumber with 15 years of experience carries far more weight than one written by a general contractor who’s only done minor repairs.
  • A health blogger who’s personally battled depression writing about "How to Cope with Seasonal Affective Disorder" has deeper credibility than someone who’s only read about it.

How Google Detects It:

  • Author bios with real-world credentials (e.g., "Certified Financial Planner," "Former ER Nurse").
  • Case studies, personal anecdotes, or testimonials from readers who’ve applied the advice.
  • Backlinks from reputable sources (e.g., industry publications, expert interviews).

2. Expertise (E)

Definition: The depth of knowledge and specialized skills a creator has in their field.

Why It Matters: Google prioritizes content from true experts—not just self-proclaimed gurus or amateurs trying to cash in on trends.

Example:

  • A detailed guide on "Advanced SEO Strategies for E-Commerce" written by a former Google Search Advocate (like John Mueller) will rank higher than a generic blog post from someone who’s only taken an online course.
  • A medical advice article on "Symptoms of Lyme Disease" should be written by a board-certified physician, not a fitness influencer who’s read a few articles.

How Google Detects It:

  • Academic degrees, certifications, or professional affiliations (e.g., "PhD in Computer Science," "Member of the American Medical Association").
  • Publications in peer-reviewed journals (for scientific or medical topics).
  • Speaking engagements, podcast appearances, or industry awards.

3. Authoritativeness (A)

Definition: The perceived credibility and influence of a creator, brand, or website in their niche.

Why It Matters: Even if someone is experienced and expert, if they’re not recognized as an authority, Google will downgrade their content’s ranking potential.

Example:

  • Neil Patel’s content on digital marketing ranks #1 for thousands of keywords because he’s widely recognized as an authority in the space.
  • A small blogger writing about "How to Start a Podcast" might rank well if they’ve built a loyal audience, but they’ll struggle against established authorities like Podcast Hosting Insider or The Podcast Host.

How Google Detects It:

  • Backlinks from high-DA (Domain Authority) sites (e.g., HuffPost, Forbes, industry-specific news outlets).
  • Mentions in major publications or media outlets.
  • Social proof (e.g., high engagement rates, shares, and citations from other experts).

4. Trustworthiness (T)

Definition: The reliability and transparency of a source, ensuring its information is accurate, up-to-date, and free from bias.

Why It Matters: Google hates misinformation—and low-trust content gets demoted fast.

Example:

  • A financial advice blog that promotes shady investment schemes will get penalized by Google, even if the author is "experienced."
  • A health website that cites outdated studies or ignores FDA warnings will lose trust signals and rank lower than a well-researched alternative.

How Google Detects It:

  • Accurate citations (e.g., studies from reputable journals, government sources, or industry reports).
  • Transparency in content creation (e.g., disclaimers, author bios, sources listed).
  • **No spammy or deceptive practices (e.g., clickbait, fake reviews, or paid promotions).

The 10 Most Powerful E-E-A-T Strategies to Rank Higher in 2024

Now that you understand what E-E-A-T is, let’s dive into 10 actionable strategies to boost your own E-E-A-T and outrank competitors.

Strategy 1: Build an Author Bio That Sells Credibility (Experience + Expertise)

Why It Works: Google scans author bios to determine if the content is credible. A weak bio (e.g., "I love writing about SEO") hurts E-E-A-T, while a strong bio (e.g., "Former Google SEO Specialist with 10+ years in technical SEO") boosts it.

Real-World Example:

  • Backlinko’s Brian Dean includes his experience as a former SEO consultant for Fortune 500 companies in his bio.
  • Neil Patel’s bios on Quick Sprout highlight his entrepreneurial background (founder of multiple agencies) and media appearances.

How to Implement: ✅ Include:

  • Your full name, title, and years of experience.
  • Relevant certifications, degrees, or awards.
  • A brief summary of your expertise (e.g., "Helped 500+ brands rank on the first page of Google").
  • A link to your LinkedIn, portfolio, or professional website.

❌ Avoid:

  • Vague statements ("I’m passionate about SEO").
  • Fake credentials (e.g., "PhD in Marketing" if you don’t have one).
  • No author photo or bio at all (Google prefers humanized content).

Example of a Strong Author Bio:

"John Smith is a certified financial planner (CFP®) with 15 years of experience helping clients navigate retirement planning. He’s been featured in The Wall Street Journal and Forbes, and his strategies have been adopted by 10+ financial advisory firms."

Strategy 2: Leverage Case Studies & Personal Anecdotes (Experience)

Why It Works: Google loves content that demonstrates real-world application. Case studies and personal stories prove you’ve actually done what you’re writing about.

Real-World Example:

  • Ahrefs’ blog often includes real case studies (e.g., "How We Grew Our Traffic by 300% in 6 Months").
  • HubSpot’s marketing blog features personal stories from their in-house experts who’ve tested and failed before finding success.

How to Implement: ✅ Add:

  • Your own failures and lessons learned (e.g., "I tried this strategy and it backfired—here’s what I did instead").
  • Client success stories (if you’re an agency).
  • Data from your own experiments (e.g., "We tested 50 backlink strategies—here’s what worked").

❌ Avoid:

  • Made-up stories (Google’s Helpful Content Update penalizes unoriginal or fabricated content).
  • Overly promotional case studies (keep it honest and transparent).

Example of a Strong Case Study:

*"Last year, I helped a local e-commerce store increase sales by 200% using user-generated content (UGC). Here’s how we did it—and why it worked better than influencer marketing."*

Strategy 3: Get Backlinks from High-Authority Sites (Authoritativeness)

Why It Works: Backlinks from trusted sources are one of the strongest E-E-A-T signals. Google sees them as votes of confidence in your expertise.

Real-World Example:

  • Neil Patel’s site gets backlinks from Forbes, Entrepreneur, and Business Insider because he’s recognized as an authority.
  • Moz’s blog ranks #1 for SEO topics partly because they’ve been featured in The New York Times and TechCrunch.

How to Implement: ✅ Build backlinks by:

  • Guest posting on reputable sites (e.g., HuffPost, Medium, industry-specific publications).
  • Getting interviewed by podcasts or news outlets.
  • **Creating link-worthy content (e.g., ultimate guides, original research, tools).
  • Leveraging HARO (Help a Reporter Out) to get quoted in articles.

❌ Avoid:

  • Buying backlinks (Google penalizes spammy links).
  • Low-quality directories or PBNs (Private Blog Networks).
  • Over-optimized anchor text (e.g., "best SEO agency"—use natural, varied links).

Example of a Strong Backlink Strategy:

"Instead of just writing a blog post, I created a free SEO audit tool and pitched it to top marketing blogs. Within 3 months, I got 15 high-DA backlinks from sites like Search Engine Journal."

Strategy 4: Cite Reputable Sources (Trustworthiness)

Why It Works: Google demands accuracy. If your content lacks citations, it loses trust signals.

Real-World Example:

  • Mayo Clinic’s health articles always cite peer-reviewed studies.
  • Investopedia’s financial guides reference government reports and economic data.

How to Implement: ✅ Always cite:

  • Government sources (e.g., CDC, IRS, FDA).
  • Peer-reviewed studies (use Google Scholar to find them).
  • Industry reports (e.g., Gartner, McKinsey, Pew Research).
  • Expert interviews (e.g., "According to Dr. Jane Smith, a cardiologist at Harvard").

❌ Avoid:

  • Outdated sources (e.g., a 2010 study on a 2024 topic).
  • Self-citations (unless you’re a true authority).
  • No sources at all (Google hates unsupported claims).

Example of Strong Citations:

"According to a 2023 study published in the Journal of Clinical Medicine, 78% of patients with chronic pain benefit from CBD oil—though more research is needed. For a deeper dive, see the FDA’s 2022 report on cannabis-derived products."

Strategy 5: Publish Original Research & Data (Expertise + Trustworthiness)

Why It Works: Original research proves you’re not just regurgitating information—you’re adding value with fresh insights.

Real-World Example:

  • Ahrefs’ "Backlink Gap Analysis Tool" is based on their own data.
  • Backlinko’s "Link Building Case Studies" are unique to their research.

How to Implement: ✅ Create:

  • Surveys (e.g., "We surveyed 1,000 marketers—here’s what they think about AI in SEO").
  • Data-driven reports (e.g., "The State of Remote Work in 2024").
  • Tools & calculators (e.g., "SEO Keyword Difficulty Checker").

❌ Avoid:

  • Stealing data from other sources without adding your own analysis.
  • Overhyping minor findings (be honest about limitations).

Example of Original Research:

*"We conducted a 6-month study tracking 100 e-commerce sites using AI-generated product descriptions. The results? Conversion rates increased by 18%—but only when combined with human editing."*

Strategy 6: Optimize for Featured Snippets & "People Also Ask" (Trustworthiness)

Why It Works: Google **prioritizes content that answers questions directly and clearly. If your content fits into a snippet, it boosts trust signals.

Real-World Example:

  • Neil Patel’s blog often answers "People Also Ask" questions in a way that qualifies as a snippet.
  • HubSpot’s guides structure answers in bullet points, making them snippet-friendly.

How to Implement: ✅ Structure content to answer:

  • "How to" questions (use step-by-step lists).
  • "Why" questions (use clear explanations).
  • "Best" questions (use comparison tables).

❌ Avoid:

  • Overly long-winded answers (Google prefers concise responses).
  • Ignoring "People Also Ask" questions (they’re huge for E-E-A-T).
